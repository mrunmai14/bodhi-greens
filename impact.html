An Honest Note

COMMON QUESTIONS

Frequently asked questions.

According to NFHS-5 (2019-21), 35.5% of Indian children under 5 are stunted due to chronic undernutrition. Additionally, 67% of childhood anaemia in India is linked to inadequate dietary diversity (UNICEF, 2022 ↗). These statistics highlight a systemic nutrition crisis that extends beyond food availability to food knowledge and behaviour.

A Theory of Change in nutrition education maps how a programme's inputs and activities produce outputs that lead to short-term, medium-term, and long-term outcomes. Bodhi Greens Foundation's Theory of Change begins with trained teachers (input), delivers nutrition sessions (activity), produces knowledge change in students (output), and aims to shift community food systems over 10–20 years (long-term impact).

M&E — Monitoring and Evaluation — is a systematic process for tracking whether a programme is working as intended. For Bodhi Greens Foundation, M&E includes tracking student enrolment, measuring teacher confidence before and after training, conducting pre/post knowledge assessments with students, observing school lunch boxes monthly, and planning independent external evaluation for Year 4.

Research and field evidence suggest that when children receive nutrition education at school, they bring new knowledge home — initiating household conversations about food that influence parental purchasing and cooking decisions. Bodhi Greens Foundation reports that 78% of parents surveyed noted positive dietary behaviour changes in the household following their child's participation in the programme (self-reported).

Bodhi Greens Foundation is transparent that its current impact figures are self-reported post-programme assessments, not independently validated. Teacher confidence (94%) and student knowledge improvement (87%) were measured through assessments administered by programme staff. Independent external evaluation is planned for Year 4 of the programme.
